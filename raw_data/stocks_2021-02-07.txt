New year brings new potential

The European Union represents the next frontier of Amarin's adventure, which has been a long and winding one with many stumbling blocks along the way. Dating back almost a decade, Amarin has found itself fighting for broad recognition and acceptance of its pure EPA drug.

Adding up the parts

Before we consider the possibilities that come with European marketing approval, let's try to break up the value of Amarin based on a sum-of-the-parts valuation. This may seem like a strange way to go about valuing a company that has only one product, but each territory of the world represents a very different opportunity and risk for the company, as well as different prospects for investors. It's important to evaluate what each region looks like now and in the coming decade in order to make a proper estimate for where the company and its stock should be valued.

If they do go it alone without a partner, it would likely take several years to make substantial inroads in the Union.

For this reason, some investors have speculated that the entire company would be sold, perhaps to a European pharma, or that they would spin off the United States section of the company separately, or that they may partner out Europe as they did with China and Canada. Because of the uncertainty, it's also difficult to value Amarin's European potential. I would offer a broad range that values Amarin's European Union presence at somewhere between $5 and $10 per share.

Partnership or expansion possibilities also exist in territories such as Latin America, Japan, Korea, and other regions. For now, I will put a conservative valuation of $.50 on these additional territories.

Projecting the share price

Altogether, when you take these different parts, it's not simple to determine where Amarin should be valued or will be valued. A lot of these uncertainties have hung over the company for the past year, as its share price has gradually recovered from spring 2020 lows around $4 and now sits just below $8 per share. The range of values based on the above breakdown suggests a share price that should fall between a low of $14 and a high of $22.

I think there is long-term value to be had, even absent a buyout. I also think it's perfectly reasonable for shares of Amarin to trade between 10 and 12 once they receive European approval, and potentially more if they come up with a creative European partnership.

Investors who are hoping for buyout numbers of 10 to 20 billion dollars ($25-50 per share) are likely to be disappointed. When the stock price peaked above $25 following FDA approval of its broader label, that's very possibly the highest the company will ever be valued. Still, a buyout between $15 and $20 per share wouldn't be out of the question, and as I mentioned earlier, organic value and growth back to double digits is quite possible over the course of 2021.

Early indications are that Vascepa could be an effective therapeutic in treating people with COVID-19, helping to prevent some of the severe disease that's seen in patients who end up hospitalized. As more data comes out of these trials, potentially later this spring, a surge in share price could follow significant results there. I would not recommend Vascepa as a COVID-19 play if you're looking at biotech stocks purely from that angle, but there is potential there.

Could there be more trouble ahead?

The downside risks here are that generic drug makers continue to flood the market. In addition to Hikma, there are three other current applications to enter the market with a generic version of Vascepa in the United States. Those are from Dr. Reddy's, Teva , and private Canadian corporation Apotex. If any or all of these companies enter the market with their own versions of Vascepa later this year, it will make for an incredibly and remarkably divided omega-3 field.

Already taking up a significant market share is a generic version of the drug Lovaza, which despite having a much different mixture of fatty acids than Vascepa, and despite not showing the cardiovascular benefits that Vascepa did within trials, nevertheless maintains substantial revenue.

The benefits shown by the REDUCE-IT trial are not fully understood. Supporters of its success point to possible anti-inflammatory effects of Vascepa, as well as the fact that it is pure EPA, whereas other omega-3 drugs contain mixtures of EPA and DHA. Will cardiologists be convinced enough of the drug's efficacy to ramp up prescriptions?

Many of the company's question marks will be answered over the next 12 months, and they present a considerable overhang for the stock. But presumably, there is a reason that in addition to Amarin, four other drug makers want to be on the market with this product in the United States.

Conclusion

Based on its current share price, Amarin looks like an intriguing value proposition. While risks remain in the form of generic competition and a challenging launch of the drug in Europe, there are several valuable opportunities as well. Now we wait to see whether Amarin management can capitalize on those opportunities for the benefit of the company and its shareholders.
